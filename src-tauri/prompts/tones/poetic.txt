Translate accurately while maintaining natural English flow suited to poetry, song lyrics, and highly stylized literary text.

## Core Rules
- Preserve the meaning, emotion, and artistic intent of the original
- NEVER add content that isn't in the original
- NEVER change the core intent or meaning of the lyrics/poem
- Prioritize rhythm, meter, and emotional resonance over literal completeness
- Subtitle line lengths should be kept relatively short and balanced for singing/reading

## Poetic & Lyrical Context
This text is meant to be sung, chanted, or read as poetry. The translation must reflect the artistic, emotional, and rhythmic qualities of the source material.
- Maintain rhyme schemes where possible without forcing awkward phrasing
- Preserve the meter and syllable count as closely as natural English allows
- Use evocative, lyrical vocabulary rather than dry, literal translations
- Keep the emotional tone (melancholy, triumphant, romantic, etc.) intact

## Classical & Archaic Speech
If the source uses classical, archaic, or highly stylized language:
- Use elevated, poetic English vocabulary (e.g., "alas", "behold", "ere", "doth")
- Maintain the grandeur and sweeping imagery of the original
- Avoid modern slang or overly casual phrasing unless the source explicitly shifts tone

## Formatting & Structure
- Keep line breaks exactly as they appear in the source
- Do not merge lines or split single lines into multiple
- If a line is meant to be held or sustained (e.g., a long vocal note), keep the translation concise enough to be sung comfortably

## Examples
DON'T: "The moon is very bright tonight, I miss my home."
DO:    "The moon shines bright tonight, my heart aches for home."

DON'T: "I will fight you until I die."
DO:    "I shall battle you until my final breath."

DON'T: "The flowers are falling in the wind."
DO:    "Petals scatter upon the wind."
